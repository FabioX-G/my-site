The word "technology" is probably the most misused and misunderstood concept by the public. In this book, retired Stanford professor W. Brian Arthur clarifies the ambiguity behind the definition of technology and how it actually works.

I will share the most important lessons I learned in this book by answering several important questions.

What is technology?

Technologies are NOT inventions that come from nowhere. They were "created — constructed, put together, assembled — from previously existing technologies" as well as from "the constant capturing and harnessing of (new) natural phenomena."

The author categorizes technology into three types:

  • Technology-singular: technology is a means to fulfill a human purpose. It originates as a new concept and develops by modifying its internal parts.

  • Technology-plural: technology as an assemblage of practices and components. It comes into being by building around certain phenomena and components and develops by changing its parts and practices.

  • Technology-general: the whole collection of all technologies that have ever existed past and present.

What is the evolution theory of technology?

Three fundamental principles constitute the theory:

  1. Combination: All technologies are combinations.

  2. Recursiveness: Each component of technology is itself in miniature a technology.

  3. Natural Phenomena: All technologies harness and exploit some effect or phenomenon, usually several.

What is the source of technological innovation?

Much like biology programs genes into myriad structures, technology programs phenomena to myriad uses.

Phenomena are the source of all technologies and the essence of technology lies in orchestrating these phenomena to fulfill a purpose.

What is the relationship between science and technology?

Technology is not simply the "applied science" like what many people think.

Science is necessary to uncover and understand deeply buried phenomena, and technology is necessary to advance science.

What qualifies as a novel technology?

To answer this, we must draw the line between an improvement and an invention.

For example, James Watt's steam engine was NOT an invention. It was an improvement of Thomas Newcomen's steam engine, which was an original invention in 1712. An improvement improves an existing technology but uses no overall new principle.

So novel technologies MUST use new principles. A principle is some effect or phenomenon in use. And these novel technologies must somehow arise through the combination of existing technologies.
