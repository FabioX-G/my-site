leading player in the emerging field of semiconductor technology, but its founder, William Shockley, was both a brilliant Nobel Prize-winning scientist and an absolute jerk to work with. After getting rejected by 35 potential investors, he arranged for them to meet with Sherman Fairchild (<em>a playboy with an inherited fortune</em>) who agreed to provide initial funding for the new company, which became known as <strong>Fairchild Semiconductor</strong>. Business Investment Company" program to subsidize startups with cheap loans and tax concessions (Mallaby, 41). However, the SBIC program ultimately failed to deliver on its promise of fueling startup growth due to a lack of oversight and accountability, as well as a focus on established businesses rather than truly innovative startups (Mallaby, 43).</p><p>Starting from 1961, Arthur Rock and Tommy Davis (with their investments made at Scientific Data Systems, Teledyne, and Intel) introduced the concept of equity-based, time-limited venture capital fund (Mallaby 44-55):</p><ul><li><p>They seeded their first venture capital fund with $100,000 of their own money and raised an additional $3.2 million from a group of about thirty "limited" partners (LPs). The LPs were primarily wealthy individuals who acted as passive investors, including six of the eight founders of Fairchild Semiconductor.</p></li><li><p>For the first time in the history of finance, Davis &amp; Rock <strong>compensated everyone</strong> in the fund, including limited and general partners, entrepreneurs, and key employees, <strong>with equity</strong> rather than cash.</p></li><li><p>By 1968, Davis &amp; Rock earned returns of <em>233x</em> and <em>22.6x</em> respectively from investments at Scientific Data Systems (their initial $257,000 later became $60 million) and at Teledyne (their initial $3.4 million later became $77 million), with each limited partner walking away with almost $10 million.</p></li></ul><p>Also in 1968, Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore, the last two of the eight traitors that founded but had not yet left Fairchild, called Arthur Rock to help raise money for their new company called <strong>Intel</strong>. The three of them bought a total of 500,000 shares for $1 each, while the outside investors kicked in $2.5 million for $5 per share, meaning that "they would control the same number of shares as the in 1976.</p></li><li><p>Jobs asked his ex-boss (from Atari) <em>Nolan Bushnell</em> to invest $50,000 for one-third of Apple.</p></li><li><p>Bushnell rejected the offer but introduced the Steves to Sequoia's <em>Don Valentine</em> (Atari's investor).</p></li><li><p>Valentine rejected to invest but introduced them to three managers who could help build Apple (while Tom Perkins and Eugene Kleiner, among other famous VCs, refused to even meet with Steve Jobs).</p></li><li><p><em>Mike Markkula</em> (one of the three managers, who knew Valentine from working at Fairchild Semiconductor) was impressed by Wozniak's computer invention and invested $91,000 for 26% of Apple.</p></li><li><p>Markkula convinced his Fairchild alumnus <em>Mike Scott</em> to quit his job to be Apple's first president.</p></li><li><p>Markkula introduced Apple to another ex-Fairchild colleague <em>Hank Smith</em>.</p></li><li><p>Hank Smith, who now worked at Venrock (the Rockefeller family venture fund), introduced Apple to pitch in front of <em>Venrock partners</em> (one of the senior partners was Peter Crisp).</p></li><li><p>Venrock invested $300,000 for 10% of Apple in 1977 (without really listening to the pitch by Steve Jobs).</p></li><li><p>Markkula introduced Apple to Andrew Grove, his ex-colleague from Intel, who soon agreed to buy a small stake in Apple.</p></li><li><p>After investments from Venrock and Grove, <em>Valentine</em> started stalking Markkula and insisted that Sequoia wants a piece of Apple (though they didn't need any money from Valentine by then).</p></li><li><p><em>Arthur Rock</em> initially refused to invest in Steve Jobs after Markkula's introduction, but after <em>Regis McKenna</em> (another connection from Markkula's valley network) whispered about Apple to Rock, Rock became interested.</p></li><li><p>Rock called <em>Dick Kramlich </em>(his ex-partner who replaced Tommy Davis) who knew Peter Crisp from Venrock (Rock previously had let Venrock into Intel's financing round in 1968, so Crisp owed Rock a favor).</p></li><li><p>Crisp then allowed $50,000 worth of Venrock's Apple allocation, which Kramlish would take $10,000 for himself and leave the rest of $40,000 to Rock.</p></li><li><p>Through Kramlich, Kramlich's British friend <em>Anthony Montagu</em> learned about Apple and asked Kramlich to call Apple's president Scott to meet with him. He then flew to California to beg Scott for allowing him to invest in Apple, but he was immediately rejected by Scott because they didn't need any extra money. the good news that Steve Wozniak just happened to want to sell $450,000 worth of his Apple equity to get a house.</p></li><li><p>To return his friend who introduced him to Apple a favor, Montagu This period was marked by Yuri Milner's investment in Facebook, Tiger Global's investments in Chinese internet platforms, and a16z's investment in Skype (Mallaby, 273-298):</p><h6>Yuri Milner and Facebook</h6><p>Russian investor Yuri Milner, who had never set foot in Silicon Valley, recognized Facebook's potential better than most Silicon Valley insiders and gained fame for his $300 million investment in the company:</p><ul><li><p>By early 2009, Facebook had already received investments from Peter Thiel, Accel, and Microsoft. The company was not seeking or accepting any additional funding. Nevertheless, Milner believed in Facebook's unrealized growth potential and flew from Russia to Palo Alto to meet with Facebook CFO Gideon Yu (and eventually Mark Zuckerberg himself). Milner made a few crucial points:</p></li></ul><ol><li><p>He argued that American VCs were wrong in thinking that Facebook, which had just reached 100 million users, had reached a saturation point. Milner had seen the growth of VKontakte, the Russian Facebook clone, and was convinced that Facebook's user base could continue to expand, especially since the site was not even among the top 5 websites in the U.S. at the time.</p></li><li><p>Milner noted that Facebook was not generating as much revenue per user as its international counterparts (Chinese social media platforms partially earned revenue from virtual gift-sending, and VKontakte's revenues per user were five times higher than Facebook's in 2009). This represented a significant opportunity.</p></li><li><p>To address Zuckerberg's strong control of Facebook, Milner promised not to take a single board seat, giving Zuckerberg as much freedom as possible.</p></li></ol><ul><li><p>In May 2009, Milner's investment company, Digital Sky Technologies (DST), purchased $200 million worth of company-issued primary stock for a 1.96% stake in Facebook, giving Zuckerberg the $10 billion pre-money valuation he wanted. DST then bought over $100 million worth of secondary employee stock, allowing employees to cash out and purchase homes and cars after being "stock option millionaires" for years.</p></li><li><p><strong>Bonanza Time</strong>: By late 2010, within 18 months, Facebook's valuation had soared from $8.6 billion to $50 billion.
